Quality: About This Data Set

Despite what Wrangler indicated and this data being from a reliable source such as the Californian government, this data seems problematic for reasons outlined below.

Is the data complete?

This dataset is not complete. A major reason is because the data leans heavily into ranges and does not give precise values for Age and Income. For instance, we can only see that a participant earns between $15,000 and $24,999. We don’t know what the precise take-home income is, whether that be $15,235 or $18,000 or $23,500. Similarly, we can only see that a participant is between 18 and 64, and we don’t know if that participant is 20 or 45. In order to feed this data into Wrangler, Excel, and Google Fusion Tables, I had to split cell ranges, but in doing so, this maintains the degree of vagueness around articulating income ranges rather than income precise values. The average minimum and maximum income levels, for example, are sketchy averages because they are averages of minimums and maximum potential values rather than actual values themselves.
It is also of note that the income intervals in the data as coded by the California Department of Finance do not seem to make much sense. For example, why is one income interval $5,000-$9,999, yet another income interval is $75,000+, and yet another income interval is $50,000-$74,000? There is no justification provided with the data on these intervals. A similar problem arises when looking at the age intervals in the original data.

Is the data coherent?

Upon first looking at the raw data, there is no reason to believe that this data is not consistent with itself. One might hypothesize that a possible risk to coherency is if the same person participated in the survey twice and reported different values in each report. Other than this, there wasno reason to assume that the specified combinations of age, income, and education attainment level can’t coexist per participant.

However, upon looking at the visualizations of the random samples after I cleaned the data with Wrangler, something is definitely wrong with either the data, the way I sampled this data, or inputting the values into Wrangler, Google Fusion Tables, or Google Spreadsheets. For instance, it does not make sense that in Random Sample B, average maximum income values for “high school or equivalent”, “no high school diploma”, and “some college, less than a 4-year degree” are well above six figures. Perhaps there are some extreme outliers there skewing the data, but even so, it is unlikely that there are extreme outliers in all these three categories within one random sample of 36 fields. If I had more time to complete this assignment, I would definitely take more random samples of this data and see what these random samples returned.

Is the data correct?

The data as indicated in these two random samples do not seem to be correct. I would need to take more random samples of the data to have a better likelihood of being right on this, but according to the Bureau of Labor Statistics, weekly median earnings do have a positive relationship with increasing levels of education obtainment. The random samples as indicated in this Byte indicate that no relationships seem to exist, yet alone a positive one. (Again, I would need to take more random samples to know this for sure.)
